CHAPTER I. INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Rationale for the Study
In the face of rapid global climate change, environmental degradation and resource depletion have necessitated a strategic global shift toward green transition and sustainable development. This transition is particularly critical for the developing economies within the ASEAN region (OECD, 2017; World Bank, 2020). For these nations, the strategic priority lies in a dual challenge: maintaining robust economic growth while simultaneously mitigating negative environmental impacts. In this context, the financial system serves as the backbone of the economy and holds a pivotal role in directing capital toward low - carbon industries and green initiatives (Campiglio, 2016; Zhang et al., 2021).

Parallel to the development of green financial markets, green bonds have emerged as an essential instrument for mobilizing medium- and long-term debt capital. These instruments are designed to finance renewable energy, green infrastructure, and climate adaptation projects, often providing a mechanism to optimize financing costs for sustainability goals (Eyraud et al., 2013; Flammer, 2021). However, within the ASEAN context - where the green bond market is still in a developing stage relative to the massive demand for capital - concerns regarding "greenwashing" have surfaced, potentially undermining the efficiency of capital mobilization (ADB, 2021; Busch & Hoffmann, 2009; Kim & Lyon, 2015).

Against this backdrop, internal capabilities and corporate governance - specifically the integration of environmental dimensions into strategy, organizational structure, and ESG disclosure - are increasingly viewed as prerequisites for the credibility of green bond issuance (Fernando et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). While the act of issuance itself represents a commitment to the market, the subsequent impact on actual corporate performance remains a complex subject of study. The interaction between a firm's signaling intent through green bonds and its substantive operational and environmental outcomes is a decisive factor in establishing long-term trust within fragmented institutional environments like ASEAN.

Based on these premises, this study is conducted under the title: "The Impact of Green Bond Issuance on Financial and Environmental Performance: Empirical Evidence from Listed Companies in ASEAN." The central objective of this research is to provide an objective evaluation system for the internal transformations of firms following green bond issuance. Specifically, the study analyzes simultaneous changes across two dimensions: internal profitability and substantive environmental stewardship. By examining this relationship, the research expects to contribute to the theoretical framework of sustainable finance while proposing practical policy implications for corporate managers and regulatory authorities in the Southeast Asian region.

1.2. Research Objectives
1.2.1. General Objective 
The primary objective of this study is to provide a rigorous causal evaluation of how green bond issuance affects the financial performance and environmental outcomes of listed companies across the ASEAN region. Through this evaluation, the research aims to establish a comprehensive evidence  -based framework to support corporate managers and regional financial regulators in fostering a sustainable, transparent, and credible financial ecosystem.

1.2.2. Specific Objectives
To achieve the general objective, the research pursues the following interconnected specific aims.
First, the study aims to establish a robust theoretical framework by systematizing existing literature on the nexus between green finance, corporate value, and environmental accountability. By analyzing these relationships through multi-dimensional theoretical lenses - including Signaling Theory, Stakeholder Theory, and the Resource-Based View (RBV) - the research constructs a comprehensive conceptual foundation to explain the mechanisms of green bond issuance. This framework elucidates how firms mitigate information asymmetry, validate their corporate legitimacy, and satisfy escalating sustainability demands within the specific institutional context of the ASEAN region.
Second, the study seeks to empirically measure the causal impacts of green bond issuance across two distinct dimensions of financial performance. This involves analyzing internal operational efficiency, represented by Return on Assets (ROA), to determine whether green financing leads to superior asset utilization or operational cost savings. Simultaneously, the research evaluates external market valuation via Tobin’s Q to capture the market’s forward-looking perception of the firm’s growth potential and risk profile following issuance. This dual-track approach ensures a holistic understanding of how green bonds influence both accounting-based and market-based metrics.
Third, the research intends to assess post-issuance environmental performance by moving beyond simplistic ESG scores. Utilizing a tiered authenticity framework, the study aims to identify whether improvements in environmental performance are substantive or merely symbolic. By analyzing potential "ESG Divergence," the research seeks to detect "greenwashing" behaviors, thereby distinguishing between firms that achieve material environmental progress and those that utilize green bonds primarily for reputational gains.
Finally, the study focuses on evaluating the persistence and statistical stability of these impacts. By employing advanced econometric techniques, such as the System GMM estimator, the research aims to control for dynamic endogeneity and unobservable firm-specific characteristics. The ultimate goal is to offer evidence-based recommendations for ASEAN corporate managers to optimize their green strategies and for policy makers to refine disclosure requirements, thereby enhancing the long-term credibility and attractiveness of the regional green bond market.

1.3. Research Questions
This thesis seeks to address a series of critical research questions that bridge the gap between financial theory and environmental practice in Southeast Asia. First, does the issuance of green bonds lead to a statistically significant improvement in the internal profitability of ASEAN listed firms, specifically as measured by the Return on Assets (ROA)? This question explores the fundamental premise of whether green financing translates into superior operational efficiency and asset utilization.
Second, how does the market value of a firm, represented by Tobin’s Q, respond to the signaling effect of a green bond issuance over time? This inquiry aims to determine whether regional investors assign a long-term "green premium" to issuing firms or if the market reaction is merely transitory.
Third, to what extent do environmental outcomes improve following bond issuance, and does the empirical evidence support a hypothesis of "material improvement" or a "greenwashing" narrative? By examining ESG Divergence, this question seeks to uncover whether firms achieve substantive environmental progress or utilize these instruments for symbolic reputational gains.
Finally, are the observed impacts of green bonds persistent and statistically stable when accounting for dynamic endogeneity and unobservable entity-fixed effects? This question investigates whether the relationship between green bond issuance and corporate performance remains significant when controlling for past performance and inherent firm-specific characteristics such as management quality and corporate culture.

1.5. Research Significance
This research holds significant value for multiple stakeholders, providing critical insights into the evolving landscape of sustainable finance in Southeast Asia. The significance of the study is outlined across three primary dimensions:
Theoretically, the study advances the existing literature by providing a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between green finance and corporate outcomes. By integrating a tiered authenticity framework to detect "greenwashing," the research moves beyond traditional binary ESG metrics. It contributes to the enrichment of Signaling and Stakeholder theories within the context of emerging markets, offering a theoretical basis for how green instruments can serve as authentic signals of corporate environmental stewardship rather than mere symbolic gestures.
Methodologically, the research addresses a common limitation in prior studies: the self-selection bias inherent in green bond issuance. By employing a multi-stage identification strategy that combines Propensity Score Matching (PSM) and Difference-in-Differences (DiD) with a System GMM estimator, the study ensures the robustness of the causal inferences. This rigorous econometric approach allows for the effective control of dynamic endogeneity and unobservable firm-specific characteristics, setting a high standard for empirical analysis in regional sustainable finance research.
Practically, the findings of this study offer vital implications for various market participants. For corporate managers in the ASEAN region, the results provide a roadmap for optimizing green investment strategies to achieve both financial viability and environmental integrity. For investors, the research provides a framework to distinguish between firms with genuine environmental commitments and those seeking mere reputational gains. Most importantly, for regional regulators and policy makers, the study offers evidence-based evidence to refine disclosure requirements and enhance the credibility of the ASEAN Green Bond Standards, ultimately attracting more international capital to the region’s green transition.
