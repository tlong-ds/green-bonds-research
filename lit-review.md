2.4. Foundational theories

2.4.1. Externality Theory and Market Failure

Market failure is an economic situation in which the free market’s allocation of goods and services is suboptimal, resulting in a net loss of economic value and requiring institutional and governmental intervention to restore societal welfare. A key catalyst of market failure is the concept of externalities. Externalities occur when an economic transaction imposes an unpriced and uncompensated cost or benefit on an unrelated third party that is not reflected in traditional market prices (Hoagland, 2021). 

The emission of greenhouse gases and the overexploitation of natural resources are prime examples of severe negative externalities. In traditional corporate operations, the entities benefiting from activities such as fossil-fuel energy production or high-emission transport do not assume the whole global costs and risks of atmospheric warming and ecological damage they cause (Helbling, n.d.). Since these ecological costs are not reflected in traditional financial accounting or market prices, it results in systemic market failures, characterised by a chronic free-market underinvestment in sustainable development.

To correct this, economic theory suggests that market-based interventions are required to internalise these negative externalities, ensuring that producers consider their societal impact (Salguero, 2025). Green financing functions as a corrective mechanism for these market inefficiencies. It operates by channelling capital towards eligible projects that yield measurable environmental benefits. Through this allocation, green bonds help to internalise the environmental costs otherwise borne by society at large. This deployment of capital minimises negative externalities and corrects market failures, facilitating both corporate green transformation and broader macroeconomic sustainability.

2.4.2. Signaling theory

Signaling theory addresses market inefficiencies caused by information asymmetry between two parties - typically an informed sender to an uninformed receiver. Such asymmetries can result in signaling inefficiencies, moral hazard, and adverse selection, as fundamental advances in information economics show. To overcome this information gap and prevent market failures like adverse selection, the informed party can engage in specific observable actions to credibly communicate their hidden high quality to the uninformed party (Mahmoud, 2025).

Significant information asymmetry exists in capital markets because investors frequently lack the detailed data required to assess factors such as a corporation’s genuine environmental commitments, its exposure to climate-related risks, or how its funds are being utilised (Loiola et al., 2025). Within this context, the issuance of a green bond functions as a strategic signaling mechanism for the firm. 

Through the issuance of green bonds, enterprises actively demonstrate their commitment to environmental protection and sustainable development (Lin et al., 2024). This signal becomes highly credible and effective when the bonds are accompanied with third-party certification and detailed environmental disclosures, which play an important role in reducing information asymmetry and reinforce investor confidence. These stringent procedures and reporting requirements are a trustworthy measure of a company's long-term environmental commitment and risk awareness since they are hard for low-quality firms to imitate without exposing themselves to scrutiny (Loiola et al., 2025). By actively signaling their environmental commitments, firms reduce information asymmetry and enhance public trust. This clear market differentiation translates into competitive financial benefits, primarily through broader funding access and the acquisition of capital at lower interest rates, known as the "Greenium." Because information asymmetry is typically more pronounced in emerging markets such as the ASEAN region, the issuance of green bonds serves as a particularly vital signalling tool for listed companies to credibly communicate their environmental quality and attract international capital.

2.4.3. Stakeholder theory

Stakeholder theory states that an organisation’s responsibilities extend beyond the traditional economic model of solely maximising shareholder wealth. Instead, firms must also acknowledge, manage, and fulfil the expectation of multiple stakeholders (Octavio et al., 2025). This framework encompasses any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the firm's operations, including foreign and institutional investors, local communities, customers, and governments. Larger companies are especially more prone to greater scrutiny from the stakeholders, which compels them to integrate environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors into their core operations (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). 

In the context of sustainable finance, issuing green bonds provides a structured, strategic route for firms to proactively respond to growing stakeholder demands for environmental protection. By allocating capital exclusively to sustainable projects, a firm explicitly aligns its operational objectives with broader socio-ecological goals. This ensures that the projects are genuinely implemented to contribute to environmental responsibility, rather than simply meeting regulatory or stakeholder expectations (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). 

From the stakeholder perspective, satisfying these environmental expectations acts as a strategic tool that builds stronger, trust-based relationships with creditors, regulators, and the community (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). Engaging stakeholders through transparent green financing allows issuers to enhance their corporate image, foster investor loyalty, and improve their long-term legitimacy. Ultimately, this increased public trust and reputational boost help to reduce perceived non-financial risks, which translates into tangible market advantages such as attracting environmentally conscious investors, strengthening stakeholder engagement, and improving overall corporate financial and environmental performance.

2.4.4. Agency theory

Agency theory focuses on the contractual design required to resolve conflicts and costs arising from the delegation of decision-making authority from principals (shareholders or owners) to agents (managers) (Hendrastuti & Harahap, 2023). This theory is based on the notion of human self-interest and constrained rationality, which emphasises that managers may not always act to maximise shareholder wealth. A primary driver of these agency problems is information asymmetry, which manifests in two main forms: adverse selection, where managers withhold critical information from outsiders, and moral hazard, where managers take opportunistic actions outside the knowledge of shareholders. As a result, principals pay "agency costs" to align managerial behaviour with their interests, which include the costs of monitoring the agent, bonding costs to ensure compliance, and the residual loss from any remaining divergent actions.

In the context of corporate sustainability, agency conflicts frequently arise when executives make suboptimal investments or participate in "greenwashing" (false environmental rhetoric) (Hendrastuti & Harahap, 2023). Managers may overinvest in low-quality corporate social responsibility programs to enhance their reputation as good citizens, resulting in wasted resources and negative impact on long-term business value.

Green bond issuance serves as a powerful contractual and governance mechanism to mitigate these specific agency problems. It serves as a powerful monitoring and bonding tool due to their strict external auditing, mandatory third-party certifications, and stringent tracking of how proceeds are used. This high level of disclosure quality directly reduces information asymmetry between managers and investors. By limiting managerial discretion and requiring transparent reporting on environmental targets, green bonds effectively minimise opportunistic behaviour, reduce agency costs, and ensure that corporate funds are efficiently allocated to genuine sustainable projects that benefit both the environment and shareholders.
2.4.5 Resource - Based View (RBV)
The Resource - Based View (RBV), most notably articulated by Barney (1991), posits that sustained competitive advantage stems from a firm's internal resources rather than market positioning. This framework assumes that strategic resources are heterogeneously distributed and immobile over time. To generate a lasting advantage, a resource must satisfy the VRIN criteria: it must be Valuable (improving efficiency), Rare (not widely accessible to competitors), Imperfectly Imitable (hard to replicate), and Non-substitutable (no equivalent alternative exists). Ultimately, RBV suggests that competitive advantage cannot be purchased on the open market; it must be cultivated from rare, proprietary resources already controlled by the firm.
Applied to green bond issuance, the RBV explains how genuine environmental commitment creates durable financial advantages. Firms cultivating robust capabilities - such as green innovation and sophisticated reporting - accumulate organizational knowledge that satisfies the VRIN criteria (Barney, 1991; Hart, 1995). These competencies are rare and difficult to imitate because they are embedded in organizational culture rather than explicit procedures, making them far superior to superficial "greenwashing" (Marquis & Qian, 2014).
Green bond issuance reinforces this framework in two ways: it provides observable evidence of a firm’s pre - existing strategic infrastructure and accelerates further capability development through dedicated capital and third - party scrutiny. Furthermore, issuance creates a unique "relational resource" by attracting long-term, sustainability-mandated investors who provide reputational legitimacy and market signaling (Flammer, 2021; Tang & Zhang, 2020). In the maturing ASEAN ecosystem, where international capital access is uneven, the first-mover advantage in building credibility with these investors constitutes a particularly durable and inimitable competitive resource (Volz, 2018). Consequently, financial benefits are most persistent for ASEAN firms that leverage green bonds to build authentic organizational infrastructure rather than those using them as a superficial label (Marquis & Qian, 2014).
2.4.6 The Porter Hypothesis
The Porter Hypothesis (Porter, 1991; Porter & van der Linde, 1995) challenges the neoclassical assumption of an inherent trade-off between environmental regulation and corporate competitiveness. Rather than viewing compliance solely as a static cost, this framework argues that well-designed environmental standards can trigger "innovation offsets" that improve resource productivity and shift the focus from pollution control to prevention. 
The application of the Porter Hypothesis to this study operates through a specific mechanism: green bond issuance, by committing firms to explicit environmental investment and third-party verification, functions as a form of voluntary self-imposed governance (Flammer, 2021; Park, 2021). This closely parallels the stimulus function that Porter and van der Linde (1995) attribute to well - designed regulation. Earmarking proceeds for projects like renewable energy and energy efficiency generates the process innovations predicted to yield resource productivity improvements (Ambec & Lanoie, 2008). If these innovations are genuine and additional, the resulting gains in input utilization should manifest in improved Return on Assets (ROA) over the medium term as projects mature (Tang & Zhang, 2020; Zhou & Cui, 2019).
Furthermore, environmental standards foster innovation offsets only when they allow industries the flexibility to choose their own technological paths (Porter & van der Linde, 1995). This principle is mirrored in the green bond market, where issuers retain discretion over project selection within eligible categories, incentivizing genuine innovation over mere compliance. Consequently, the Porter Hypothesis predicts a positive impact on financial performance concentrated among firms deploying proceeds toward high-impact projects. This effect is expected to materialize over a multi-year horizon - a temporal dynamic captured by this study’s ten-year observation window and lagged variable specification.
2.4.7 The Concept of "Greenium" (Green Premium)
The "greenium" (green premium) represents a yield advantage where green bonds command lower interest rates than comparable conventional bonds, driven by investor demand for environmental attributes (Zerbib, 2019). This phenomenon serves as a primary financial incentive for issuers, with empirical evidence from Gianfrate and Peri (2019) suggesting primary market premiums as high as -18.5 basis points. While some scholars argue that financial benefits accrue through equity channels - such as improved reputational signaling and investor base quality - rather than direct debt subsidies (Tang & Zhang, 2020; Flammer, 2021), the theoretical core of the greenium remains rooted in two pillars: non-pecuniary investor preferences and risk reduction from mitigating transition and regulatory threats.
In the ASEAN context, these mechanisms face unique dynamics. While the regional ESG investor base is less developed than in Europe - potentially limiting demand-driven premiums - the regulatory transition risk is increasingly significant. As ASEAN jurisdictions rapidly align with Paris Agreement commitments, the risk-reduction component of the greenium may become the dominant driver of financial performance, offering issuers a strategic hedge against tightening environmental standards
2.5. Green Bonds and Environmental Performance

2.5.1. Mechanism of Impact

The primary mechanism through which green bonds improve corporate environmental performance is based on the binding concept of a "green commitment". Unlike conventional financing, the issuance of green bonds requires that the raised capital be strictly allocated to eligible green projects, such as renewable energy, pollution prevention, and sustainable water management (ICMA, 2025). This targeted deployment of capital forces the organisation to undertake a structural green transformation. By allocating funds to research and development (R&D) and the generation of green innovations, firms actively implement clean technologies that reduce greenhouse gas emissions and long-term energy consumption as evidenced by an increase in green patent applications (Luo & Chan, 2024). 

To ensure these commitments materialise into tangible ecological benefits and to mitigate the risks of "greenwashing", the issuance process relies heavily on structured goal-setting and stringent external validation. Companies are strongly encouraged to set specific, quantitative environmental targets before issuing, such as Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) (Bai, 2025). Additionally, issuers usually subject themselves to independent pre-issuance external reviews and third-party certifications, such as those associated with the International Capital Market Association (ICMA) or the Climate Bonds Initiative (CBI), to verify the environmental integrity of the bonds (ICMA, 2025). These independent external reviewers monitor the project evaluation, selection, and management processes, ensuring the funds are genuinely allocated to sustainability objectives.

Following issuance, this mechanism of impact is maintained through ongoing internal reporting and external supervision. Issuers must provide transparent annual impact reports on the qualitative and quantitative environmental outcomes of their financed projects. Simultaneously, the public nature of green bond issuances attracts heightened scrutiny from the media and financial analysts (Luo & Chan, 2024). This increased visibility serves as an effective external monitoring mechanism. In the face of intense media and analyst attention, corporate management is under constant pressure to strictly fulfil their environmental commitments and maintain their social legitimacy, ensuring that the initial capital raise translates into sustained improvements in corporate environmental performance.

2.5.2. H1: Green bond issuance has a positive impact on the environmental performance of companies in ASEAN countries.

This hypothesis assumes that strict governance, targeted capital deployment, and reporting standards associated with green bonds result in tangible environmental improvements. Green bonds, unlike traditional financial instruments, show a deliberate corporate shift towards a low-carbon economy, with raised funds explicitly allocated to projects that benefit the environment and mitigate climate change (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). 

Recent empirical studies focusing on listed companies in the ASEAN region provide strong evidence for this positive impact. The data shows that improved environmental, social, and governance (ESG) ratings after green bond issuance reflect genuine corporate responsibility rather than deceptive marketing or superficial rebranding (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). In fact, firms that actively engage in the green bond market and achieve higher ESG scores are shown to be significantly less likely to engage in "greenwashing" behaviour. Instead, their financial commitments are successfully translated into real-world operational changes that reduce their environmental impact.

Evidence from neighbouring Asian markets further supports the specific mechanisms driving this positive environmental trajectory. The issuance of green bonds has been proven to effectively promote corporate environmental performance by stimulating increased research and development (R&D) investment and fostering green innovation within the firm (Luo & Chan, 2024). Furthermore, these tangible ecological benefits are particularly pronounced and realised more rapidly when issuing companies establish specific, quantitative targets for reducing greenhouse gas emissions or energy consumption prior to securing the capital (Bai, 2025). As a result, the strategic allocation of green financing effectively drives structural transformations that improve overall corporate environmental performance throughout the region.

2.6. Green Bonds and Financial Performance

2.6.1. Mechanism of Impact

The mechanisms through which green bonds influence corporate financial performance are primarily driven by funding cost advantages, enhanced signalling, and operational efficiency improvements.

First, green bonds allow enterprises to broaden their financing channels and increase funding flexibility (Lin et al., 2024). By issuing these instruments, companies can gain access to a rapidly expanding pool of institutional investors who are specifically required to hold sustainable and environmental, social, and governance (ESG) assets. This high market demand frequently generates a pricing advantage known as the "Greenium" (green premium), enabling issuers to secure capital at lower interest rates than conventional bonds (Loiola et al., 2025). Ultimately, this reduces the firm's overall cost of debt and eases financing constraints, freeing up cash flow for further productive investments (Bai, 2025). 

Second, green bonds rely on a powerful signalling and transparency mechanism. The issuance process requires strict adherence with use-of-proceeds tracking, independent third-party certifications, and ongoing environmental impact reporting (Loiola et al., 2025). Particularly in emerging markets like the ASEAN region, where institutional risks and information asymmetry can be pronounced, these rigorous governance and environmental disclosures act as a highly credible signal of a firm's quality and risk awareness (Zhang, 2025). This proactive signalling reduces perceived investment risks, fosters investor loyalty, and builds long-term stakeholder trust, which frequently translates into positive market valuation and increased corporate legitimacy.

Finally, the targeted deployment of green bond proceeds directly enhances internal operational efficiency. By dedicating capital exclusively to clean technologies, energy-saving measures, and green innovation, the firm is forced to undergo a structural green transformation (Lin et al., 2024). Over time, these sustainable investments significantly reduce resource consumption, environmental pollution, and exposure to future regulatory and legal risks. The combination of reduced environmental compliance costs and improved internal asset utilisation establishes a more stable and sustainable operational foundation, directly driving up accounting profitability metrics, such as Return on Assets (ROA).

2.6.2. H2: Green bond issuance has a positive impact on the financial performance of companies in ASEAN countries.

This hypothesis assumes that the strategic, reputational, and operational benefits of issuing green bonds will ultimately outweigh the initial costs of transition and compliance. Empirical literature confirms that green bond issuance is generally associated with improved firm value, profitability, and operational performance over the long run (Loiola et al., 2025). Notably, emerging markets typically experience more positive financial effects than developed economies.

However, the implications for traditional accounting metrics can be multifaceted. In the short term, environmental initiatives and the initial investments required for eco-friendly practices may impose financial burdens that temporarily reduce accounting-based performance metrics such as Return on Assets (ROA). Despite this short-term cost, market-based valuations such as Tobin's Q frequently see significant enhancements, as investors perceive these long-term environmental strategies as credible signals of proactive risk management and strategic foresight (Tripopsakul, 2025).

Furthermore, the magnitude of these financial benefits is not uniform and depends heavily on firm-specific features, particularly structural factors such as firm size, pre-existing leverage, and current liquidity levels. For example, cash-constrained enterprises stand to realise substantial benefits by unlocking new green financing channels, which broadens their funding sources and significantly eases their financing constraints (Lin et al., 2024). Conversely, cash-rich firms or those issuing green debt without substantive environmental improvements frequently face heightened market scepticism regarding "greenwashing", an issue that can backfire and inadvertently increase their financing costs (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). Therefore, the financial impact of green bonds is strongly moderated by the issuer's initial financial standing and the broader institutional context in which they operate. Additionally, the most significant enhancements in key financial metrics, such as Return on Assets (ROA) and Tobin's Q, are observed primarily among firms that move beyond symbolic rhetoric to establish specific, quantitative environmental targets (Bai, 2025).

Ultimately, ASEAN companies can obtain a pricing advantage, often referred to as the "Greenium", that significantly reduces their total cost of debt when supported by these sincere environmental commitments and open disclosures (Loiola et al., 2025). By accepting lower yields, investors financially reward the positive environmental impact generated by the underlying projects. Simultaneously, engaging in this rigorous issuance process sends a highly credible signal to the market, highlighting the firm's long-term resilience, proactive risk management, and high organisational quality (Bai, 2025).

3. Literature Review
3.1 Overview of Previous Studies
3.1.1 Green Bonds and Environmental Performance
The primary justification for the green bond market is its capacity to channel capital toward projects yielding verifiable environmental benefits (Baker et al., 2018). Scholarly interest has focused on whether the "green" label translates into substantive ecological improvements or merely serves as a tool for "greenwashing."
Significant empirical support for the substantive impact of green bonds is provided by Flammer (2021), who utilized a large-scale international sample to demonstrate that green bond issuance leads to improved environmental ratings and a measurable reduction in carbon emission intensity. This suggests that the bond's restrictive covenants and the "signaling" effect to stakeholders compel firms to improve environmental stewardship. Similarly, Zhou and Cui (2019) found that in the Chinese market, green bonds significantly enhance Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) performance, particularly in terms of green innovation and waste reduction.
However, the literature also warns of "symbolic compliance." Marquis and Qian (2014) argue that firms facing high institutional pressure may engage in reporting that lacks substantive operational change. This is manifested in the "greenwashing" debate, where firms may use proceeds to refinance existing projects without creating "additionality" - environmental benefits that would not have occurred absent the bond (Tang & Zhang, 2020). Furthermore, Al Breiki and Nobanee (2019) emphasize that only corporations engaging in voluntary, in-depth disclosures truly attain sustainability objectives, highlighting the gap between mere issuance and actual performance.
3.1.2 Green Bonds and Financial Performance
The financial consequences of issuance are traditionally analyzed through two lenses: the cost of debt (the "Greenium") and overall firm profitability or market value.
Regarding the cost of debt, Zerbib (2019) and Gianfrate and Peri (2019) provide evidence of a "greenium" - a yield discount reflecting incremental investor demand for ESG-aligned assets. Beyond the cost of debt, the "strong" version of the Porter Hypothesis (Porter & van der Linde, 1995) suggests that environmental commitments trigger innovation offsets that enhance resource productivity, eventually manifesting in higher Return on Assets (ROA) (Ambec & Lanoie, 2008). Tang and Zhang (2020) also found that issuance leads to positive stock market reactions and improved long-term institutional ownership, which enhances Tobin’s Q.
Conversely, some studies suggest that administrative costs (including certification and ongoing disclosure) may offset gains. Maltais and Nykvist (2020) found that in developed markets like Sweden, green bonds are often "financially neutral," serving branding purposes rather than direct profit maximization. This highlights that financial impact is context-dependent, varying by industry and market maturity.
3.2 Research Gaps and Originality of the Study
Despite the rapid proliferation of green bond literature, several critical gaps remain that limit the generalizability of existing findings, particularly for emerging economies. The first significant gap concerns geographic concentration. The vast majority of current empirical evidence is derived from mature markets such as the United States and Europe, or large-scale transitions in China (Flammer, 2021; Zhou & Cui, 2019). Consequently, there is a conspicuous lack of longitudinal, multi-country studies focusing on the ASEAN region. As Volz (2018) observes, ASEAN presents a unique "middle-ground" ecosystem where environmental regulations are tightening rapidly in alignment with international accords, yet the local ESG investor infrastructure remains in a maturing phase. This study fills this void by providing a comprehensive analysis of listed companies across the ASEAN bloc, offering a localized perspective on the Resource-Based View (RBV) and the unique first-mover advantages available to firms in this rapidly evolving region.
Furthermore, this research addresses the persistent challenge of self-selection and endogeneity that plagues many earlier studies. A common limitation in the literature is the reliance on simple OLS regressions, which often fail to account for the fact that high-performing firms with superior existing capabilities are inherently more likely to issue green bonds (Flammer, 2021; Tang & Zhang, 2020). This leads to a selection bias where observed performance improvements may be inherent to the firm’s quality rather than a direct result of the bond issuance. Following the foundational framework of Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983), this study introduces superior methodological rigor by combining Propensity Score Matching (PSM) with Difference-in-Differences (DiD) and System GMM. By constructing a credible counterfactual and utilizing lagged control variables, this research isolates the causal effect of issuance, a level of econometric depth often missing in emerging market studies.
Another novel contribution of this study is the implementation of a "Greenwashing" filter to verify the authenticity of environmental commitments. While most existing research takes the "green" label at face value, this study acknowledges the risks of selective disclosure and symbolic compliance (Kim & Lyon, 2015). To distinguish between purely "symbolic" gestures and "substantive" operational changes, we introduce an "ESG Divergence Proxy" (Marquis & Qian, 2014). By categorizing issuers into tiers based on the statistical significance of post-issuance ESG improvements and verified third-party certifications (CBI/ICMA), this research adds a critical layer of verification regarding the actual impact of green finance that is currently absent from the ASEAN-specific literature.
Finally, this study addresses the limitation of short-term temporal dynamics prevalent in current event studies. The Porter Hypothesis (1995) posits that "innovation offsets" - the improvements in resource productivity that drive financial gains - require a multi-year horizon to fully materialize. As noted by Ambec et al. (2013), the failure of many studies to find a "strong" Porter effect is frequently a consequence of insufficient observation windows. By utilizing a ten-year observation window (2015–2024) and a lagged variable specification (Klassen & McLaughlin, 1996), this study provides a realistic assessment of how green projects mature into tangible financial assets. This longitudinal approach allows for the capture of medium-to-long-term performance shifts that short-term market reactions inevitably overlook.
