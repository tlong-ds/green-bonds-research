CHAPTER II. LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
2.1. Key Concepts
2.1.1. Definition of Green Bonds
According to Green Bond Principles of the International Capital Market Association, green bonds are any type of bond instrument where the proceeds or an equivalent amount will be exclusively applied to finance or re-finance, in part or in full, new and/or existing eligible Green Projects.  Green bonds are specialized fixed-income securities for projects with positive environmental or climatic outcomes, with instruments that facilitate the financing of sustainable initiatives, including renewable energy infrastructure, carbon-neutral transport, resource efficiency, and the restoration of biodiversity (Baker et al., 2018). 

The Green Bond Principles (GBP) categorize green bonds into four distinct types—Standard Green Use of Proceeds Bonds, Green Revenue Bonds, Green Project Bonds, and Secured Green Bonds—which are distinguished by their specific debt structures, the nature of the investor's recourse to the issuer, and the source of repayment, such as pledged cash flows or specific green assets. 

For investors, green bonds allow investors to support verified environmental projects while maintaining a risk profile identical to the issuer’s conventional debt. Because these instruments typically grant recourse to the issuer’s entire balance sheet, the investor is insulated from the specific operational or financial risks associated with the underlying green assets (Maltais and Nykvist, 2020). Additionally, green bonds help mitigate information asymmetry by providing a credible signal of a company's commitment to climate-neutral operations, especially when the bonds are verified by third-party certification (Yeow & Ng, 2021; Khurram et al., 2023). For issuers, the primary benefit of green bonds is the "green advantage" or premium (often called a "greenium"), which allows them to raise capital at a lower cost than conventional bonds, as research indicates that issuers pay an average of 18 basis points less in annual interest, a saving that persists in both primary and secondary markets (Gianfrate & Peri, 2019). Beyond direct interest savings, green bonds can significantly enhance corporate value and innovation performance, particularly as firms use the proceeds to fund research and development for sustainable technologies (Khurram et al., 2023).

Despite their growth, green bonds present several disadvantages for both issuers and the broader financial market. For issuers, the primary drawbacks are the additional transaction costs and administrative burdens associated with the need to certify, monitor, and report on the "green" use of proceeds (Maltais & Nykvist, 2020). These requirements can create significant overhead, and the market still suffers from ambiguity regarding what projects truly qualify as green, leading to the potential for "greenwashing" where firms misuse the label for publicity without achieving real environmental impact (Yeow & Ng, 2021; Maltais & Nykvist, 2020).

2.1.2. Definition of Corporate Environmental Performance
At the national level, frameworks such as the Environmental Performance Index (EPI) measure outcomes through two primary objectives: protecting human health and maintaining ecosystem vitality (Hsu & Zomer, 2016). Performance is assessed across nine key policy categories, including air quality, water and sanitation, biodiversity, and climate and energy, using proximity-to-target indicators that benchmark data against international agreements and scientific standards (Hsu & Zomer, 2016). This approach is designed to move environmental policy debates toward data-driven accountability by tracking specific outcomes rather than just process-oriented metrics (Hsu & Zomer, 2016).
At the organizational and operational levels, environmental performance is commonly evaluated through a four-dimensional framework comprising organizational systems, stakeholder relations, external impacts, and regulatory compliance (Jiménez & Lorente, 2001; Ilinitch et al., 1998). Organizational systems criteria focus on internal processes like the existence of a board-level environmental committee, the integration of environmental performance into executive compensation, and the use of auditing and Total Quality Environmental Management (TQEM) programs (Ilinitch et al., 1998). While stakeholder relations are assessed through indicators such as the disclosure of environmental capital expenditures and interactions with external groups, external impacts focus on quantifiable environmental outcomes like toxic release volumes and hazardous spills (Jiménez & Lorente, 2001; Ilinitch et al., 1998). Finally, regulatory compliance measures adherence to legal standards, often tracked by the frequency of governmental proceedings and the magnitude of fines assessed (Ilinitch et al., 1998)

2.1.3. Definition of Corporate Financial Performance
Accounting-based indicators, primarily Return on Assets (ROA) and Return on Equity (ROE), are frequently employed to evaluate the firm's internal profitability and management efficiency in utilizing capital following green bond issuance (Molina-Azorín et al., 2009; Yeow & Ng, 2021). Specifically, ROA measures how effectively a firm generates profit relative to its total assets, while metrics like asset turnover and gross profit margins provide insights into operational efficiency and the productive use of resources (Yeow & Ng, 2021).

Furthermore, market-based criteria such as Tobin’s Q and stock market returns are utilized to capture investor sentiment and the long-term market valuation of the firm's green transition (Molina-Azorín et al., 2009; Yeow & Ng, 2021). Beyond these standard indicators, researchers also examine debt-specific criteria, including yield spreads and the potential for a "greenium"—where green bonds may offer lower interest costs than conventional debt—to assess the direct economic impact on the issuer's cost of capital (Yeow & Ng, 2021; Xu et al., 2020).

2.2. Theoretical Framework
2.2.1. Stakeholder Theory
Stakeholder theory states that an organisation’s responsibilities extend beyond the traditional economic model of solely maximising shareholder wealth. Instead, firms must also acknowledge, manage, and fulfil the expectation of multiple stakeholders (Octavio et al., 2025). This framework encompasses any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the firm's operations, including foreign and institutional investors, local communities, customers, and governments. Larger companies are especially more prone to greater scrutiny from the stakeholders, which compels them to integrate environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors into their core operations (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). 

In the context of sustainable finance, issuing green bonds provides a structured, strategic route for firms to proactively respond to growing stakeholder demands for environmental protection. By allocating capital exclusively to sustainable projects, a firm explicitly aligns its operational objectives with broader socio-ecological goals. This ensures that the projects are genuinely implemented to contribute to environmental responsibility, rather than simply meeting regulatory or stakeholder expectations (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). 

From the stakeholder perspective, satisfying these environmental expectations acts as a strategic tool that builds stronger, trust-based relationships with creditors, regulators, and the community (Crecia Viona et al., 2025). Engaging stakeholders through transparent green financing allows issuers to enhance their corporate image, foster investor loyalty, and improve their long-term legitimacy. Ultimately, this increased public trust and reputational boost help to reduce perceived non-financial risks, which translates into tangible market advantages such as attracting environmentally conscious investors, strengthening stakeholder engagement, and improving overall corporate financial and environmental performance.
2.2.2. Signaling Theory
Signaling theory addresses market inefficiencies caused by information asymmetry between two parties - typically an informed sender to an uninformed receiver. Such asymmetries can result in signaling inefficiencies, moral hazard, and adverse selection, as fundamental advances in information economics show. To overcome this information gap and prevent market failures like adverse selection, the informed party can engage in specific observable actions to credibly communicate their hidden high quality to the uninformed party (Mahmoud, 2025).

Significant information asymmetry exists in capital markets because investors frequently lack the detailed data required to assess factors such as a corporation’s genuine environmental commitments, its exposure to climate-related risks, or how its funds are being utilised (Loiola et al., 2025). Within this context, the issuance of a green bond functions as a strategic signaling mechanism for the firm. 

Through the issuance of green bonds, enterprises actively demonstrate their commitment to environmental protection and sustainable development (Lin et al., 2024). This signal becomes highly credible and effective when the bonds are accompanied with third-party certification and detailed environmental disclosures, which play an important role in reducing information asymmetry and reinforce investor confidence. These stringent procedures and reporting requirements are a trustworthy measure of a company's long-term environmental commitment and risk awareness since they are hard for low-quality firms to imitate without exposing themselves to scrutiny (Loiola et al., 2025). By actively signaling their environmental commitments, firms reduce information asymmetry and enhance public trust. This clear market differentiation translates into competitive financial benefits, primarily through broader funding access and the acquisition of capital at lower interest rates, known as the "Greenium." Because information asymmetry is typically more pronounced in emerging markets such as the ASEAN region, the issuance of green bonds serves as a particularly vital signalling tool for listed companies to credibly communicate their environmental quality and attract international capital.
2.2.3. Resource-Based View (RBV)
The Resource - Based View (RBV), most notably articulated by Barney (1991), posits that sustained competitive advantage stems from a firm's internal resources rather than market positioning. This framework assumes that strategic resources are heterogeneously distributed and immobile over time. To generate a lasting advantage, a resource must satisfy the VRIN criteria: it must be Valuable (improving efficiency), Rare (not widely accessible to competitors), Imperfectly Imitable (hard to replicate), and Non-substitutable (no equivalent alternative exists). Ultimately, RBV suggests that competitive advantage cannot be purchased on the open market; it must be cultivated from rare, proprietary resources already controlled by the firm.
Applied to green bond issuance, the RBV explains how genuine environmental commitment creates durable financial advantages. Firms cultivating robust capabilities - such as green innovation and sophisticated reporting - accumulate organizational knowledge that satisfies the VRIN criteria (Barney, 1991; Hart, 1995). These competencies are rare and difficult to imitate because they are embedded in organizational culture rather than explicit procedures, making them far superior to superficial "greenwashing" (Marquis & Qian, 2014).
Green bond issuance reinforces this framework in two ways: it provides observable evidence of a firm’s pre - existing strategic infrastructure and accelerates further capability development through dedicated capital and third - party scrutiny. Furthermore, issuance creates a unique "relational resource" by attracting long-term, sustainability-mandated investors who provide reputational legitimacy and market signaling (Flammer, 2021; Tang & Zhang, 2020). In the maturing ASEAN ecosystem, where international capital access is uneven, the first-mover advantage in building credibility with these investors constitutes a particularly durable and inimitable competitive resource (Volz, 2018). Consequently, financial benefits are most persistent for ASEAN firms that leverage green bonds to build authentic organizational infrastructure rather than those using them as a superficial label (Marquis & Qian, 2014).
2.3. Literature Review
2.3.1. Impact of Green Bonds on Financial Performance
The relationship between green bond (GB) issuance and corporate financial performance remains a subject of intense debate, with empirical results often diverging due to differences in methodological approaches and market contexts. According to Signaling Theory, green bonds serve as a positive signal of a firm's commitment to sustainability, helping to mitigate information asymmetry and allowing the firm to access capital at more favorable rates. Bai (2025), employing Propensity Score Matching combined with Difference-in-Differences (PSM-DiD) on a dataset of Japanese listed firms, provides strong evidence that GB issuance significantly improves both Return on Assets (ROA) and Tobin’s Q in the long run. These results suggest that the market recognizes the intrinsic value of green bonds when they are aligned with strategic corporate objectives. Similarly, Lemos (2025), applying the PSM-DiD method to the European real estate sector, found a positive impact on market performance, although accounting-based profitability (ROE) did not show immediate changes. This suggests that the financial benefits of green bonds are often reflected early through investor expectations and market valuation before manifesting as bottom-line profits in financial statements.
Conversely, a different strand of literature offers more cautious or even negative results when applying stricter controls for endogeneity. Hoang et al. (2020) utilized a System GMM model to address the bidirectional relationship between financial performance and bond issuance, finding that for European firms, the actual volume of green bonds issued was inversely correlated with ROA and ROE. The authors argue that the high costs of compliance, green auditing fees, and the pressure of immediate investment in new green technologies may outweigh the benefits of reduced interest rates in the short term. Supporting this view, Yeow & Ng (2021) conducted a cross-country analysis using PSM-DiD and concluded that there were no statistically significant differences in financial performance between green bond issuers and conventional bond issuers after controlling for firm characteristics. The contradiction between these studies (e.g., Bai finding a positive effect while Yeow & Ng find a neutral one) emphasizes that the financial efficacy of green bonds is highly sensitive to the institutional quality of the specific region. Specifically, in the ASEAN context, Viona et al. (2026) employed a Random-effects GLS model and indicated that financial benefits tend to be indirect, primarily helping firms mitigate systemic risks and improve credit reputation rather than generating immediate profit surges.


2.3.2. Green Bonds and Environmental Performance
Environmental impact is the core metric for evaluating the legitimacy of green bonds; however, modern research has highlighted a profound decoupling between "disclosure effectiveness" (information in reports) and "operational effectiveness" (actual emission reductions). Studies supporting a positive impact typically focus on quantitative operational indicators. Bai (2025) used a DiD model to demonstrate that in Japan, GB issuance leads to a substantive decline in Greenhouse Gas (GHG) intensity and energy consumption levels. Notably, this research emphasizes a critical comparative point: environmental performance improvements are only truly significant in firms that establish specific quantitative targets. Concurring with this perspective, Ordonez-Borrallo et al. (2024) integrated the Attention-based View with an Instrumental Variables (2SLS) approach to assert that green bonds force managers to direct their focus toward green KPIs, thereby creating structural changes in the firm’s production processes.
On the other hand, a group of scholars expresses skepticism regarding the phenomenon of "greenwashing," particularly in emerging markets. Hoang et al. (2020), through panel data regression, discovered a paradox: while GB issuance significantly increases ESG disclosure scores, it has an insignificant relationship with actual emission reductions. This reflects a "decoupling" strategy, where firms use green bonds as a public relations tool to polish their image without altering the nature of their operations. In the ASEAN region, Viona et al. (2026) utilized Logistics and GLS models to show that while green bonds help improve overall environmental scores, the "greenwashing puzzle" remains a major challenge due to the lack of stringent independent monitoring mechanisms.
When comparing and contrasting the aforementioned studies, there is a clear methodological shift from static models (such as the GLS used by Viona et al., 2026) toward robust causal and endogeneity-controlled methods like PSM-DiD and System GMM (Bai, 2025; Hoang et al., 2020). The divergence in results among authors often stems from the choice of dependent variables: studies using "Composite ESG Scores" (Viona et al., 2026; Yeow & Ng, 2021) tend to find positive symbolic impacts, whereas those delving into "Quantitative Operational Indicators" (Bai, 2025; Hoang et al., 2020) show more nuanced and polarized results. This confirms that to authentically assess the impact of green bonds in ASEAN, research must combine advanced econometric models to handle endogeneity with substantive environmental metrics like GHG intensity. The gap created by the conflict between the "substantive performance" seen in Japan and the "symbolic performance" often noted in ASEAN provides the foundation for this study to establish more rigorous testing hypotheses.

2.4. Research Gaps and Research Novelty
Despite the growing body of literature on sustainable finance, this study identifies four critical research gaps that remain unresolved, particularly within the unique institutional landscape of the ASEAN region. First, the lack of granularity in assessing "Environmental Authenticity" versus "Greenwashing" in ASEAN. While Viona et al. (2026) provide evidence that green bonds correlate with higher composite ESG scores in ASEAN, these scores are often criticized for being opaque and easily manipulated. Existing regional studies have yet to move beyond third-party ESG ratings to analyze substantive environmental outcomes. By utilizing direct operational metrics such as Greenhouse Gas (GHG) intensity and Energy Consumption intensity, this research addresses the "Greenwashing Puzzle" identified by Khan & Vismara (2025), distinguishing between symbolic market signaling and genuine ecological transition.
Second, the unexplored role of specific quantitative environmental targets. A pioneering study by Bai (2025) on Japanese firms revealed that green bonds only deliver significant performance improvements when issuers set specific, quantitative environmental targets (e.g., precise CO2 reduction percentages). In the ASEAN context, where disclosure standards are still maturing and fragmented, the necessity of these quantitative commitments remains an unexplored "black box." This study fills this gap by examining whether the presence of such targets acts as a prerequisite for the success of green bond issuance in Southeast Asia.
Third, the persistence of the "Financial Impact Paradox." There is a profound contradiction in current empirical findings. While some scholars find a significant "greenium" and improved financial indicators like ROA and Tobin’s Q (Bai, 2025; Flammer, 2021), others such as Yeow & Ng (2021) - report negligible or non-existent financial gains. This divergence suggests that the financial outcome of green bonds is highly sensitive to institutional quality and market maturity. By focusing exclusively on the ASEAN bloc, this study provides a localized explanation for these conflicting results, accounting for the region's specific regulatory frameworks and investor behaviors.
Drawing upon the aforementioned research gaps, this study is meticulously designed to provide a more comprehensive and rigorous examination of the impacts of green bond issuance within the ASEAN region. Rather than relying solely on subjective composite ESG scores, this research pioneers the use of direct operational metrics such as Greenhouse Gas (GHG) intensity and energy consumption intensity, to confront the “greenwashing puzzle” head-on. Furthermore, by integrating specific quantitative environmental targets into the analytical model, the study aims to decode the signaling mechanism through which green bonds transition from symbolic commitments into substantive financial and environmental performance. By employing an advanced methodological framework that combines PSM-DiD with System GMM, this research not only addresses the technical shortcomings of prior studies but also provides a robust empirical foundation for managers and policymakers in fostering a credible sustainable financial market in Southeast Asia.



## 2.5. Research Hypotheses

Drawing on stakeholder theory, signaling theory, and the resource-based view, and informed by the empirical patterns documented in prior literature, we propose the following formal hypotheses to guide this investigation:

### H1: Environmental Performance Impact

**H1a:** Green bond issuance is positively associated with improvements in ESG scores.

*Theoretical basis:* Stakeholder theory posits that green bond issuance represents a firm's response to stakeholder pressure for environmental accountability (Flammer, 2021). Signaling theory further suggests that transparent environmental commitments reduce information asymmetry and enhance credibility (Tang & Zhang, 2020). Prior evidence from Japan (Bai, 2025) and Europe (Ordonez-Borrallo et al., 2024) supports positive ESG effects when firms make verifiable commitments.

**H1b:** Green bond issuance is negatively associated with emissions intensity (GHG emissions per unit revenue).

*Theoretical basis:* The resource-based view suggests that green bond proceeds directed toward operational improvements (e.g., renewable energy, efficiency upgrades) create firm-specific capabilities that reduce emissions (Hart, 1995). However, the "greenwashing puzzle" (Hoang et al., 2020) suggests this effect may be context-dependent, particularly in emerging markets with weaker monitoring infrastructure.

### H2: Financial Performance Impact

**H2a:** Green bond issuance is positively associated with accounting profitability (ROA).

*Theoretical basis:* If green bonds reduce information asymmetry and attract lower-cost capital (signaling theory), and if proceeds are deployed to enhance operational efficiency (RBV), profitability should improve (Bai, 2025). However, competing evidence suggests short-term compliance costs may offset benefits (Hoang et al., 2020), making this an empirical question in the ASEAN context.

**H2b:** Green bond issuance is positively associated with firm valuation (Tobin's Q).

*Theoretical basis:* Even absent immediate accounting gains, market-based valuation may reflect investor expectations of future benefits or reduced tail risks (Flammer, 2021; Lemos, 2025). The "greenium" literature suggests equity markets reward environmental commitments independently of current cash flows.

**H2c:** Green bond issuance is negatively associated with the cost of debt (interest expense / total debt).

*Theoretical basis:* The existence of a "greenium" in bond markets (Gianfrate & Peri, 2019; Larcker & Watts, 2020) implies that green bond issuers should secure subsequent debt at lower rates, reflecting enhanced creditworthiness and stakeholder trust.

### H3: Certification and Authenticity Effects

**H3a:** Bonds with third-party certification (CBI/ICMA) demonstrate stronger environmental performance improvements than self-labeled green bonds.

*Theoretical basis:* Certification reduces information asymmetry more credibly than self-labeling (Fatica & Panzica, 2021) and imposes reputational costs on issuers who fail to deliver (Bachelet et al., 2019), thus strengthening the signaling mechanism.

**H3b:** Bonds with higher authenticity scores (combining ESG performance, certification, and issuer credibility) demonstrate stronger positive effects on both environmental and financial outcomes.

*Theoretical basis:* Authenticity captures the alignment between stated environmental intent and demonstrated capability (RBV). High-authenticity issuers possess the organizational infrastructure to convert green financing into real operational improvements, whereas low-authenticity issuers engage in symbolic compliance (Marquis & Qian, 2014).

### H4: Moderating Effects

**H4:** The positive effects of green bond issuance on environmental and financial performance are stronger for larger firms.

*Theoretical basis:* Stakeholder theory predicts that larger firms face greater scrutiny and reputational pressure, incentivizing genuine compliance (Viona et al., 2025). Larger firms also possess greater absorptive capacity and economies of scale in implementing green technologies (RBV), though they may also incur higher reporting and compliance costs.

These hypotheses guide the empirical investigation in Chapters 3 and 4, where we employ PSM-DiD and System GMM to isolate causal effects while controlling for selection bias and dynamic endogeneity.
