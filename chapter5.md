# CHAPTER V. CONCLUSIONS

## 5.1. Research Summary

This study examined the causal impact of green bond issuance on the financial and environmental performance of listed companies across six ASEAN member states (Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia, and Philippines) from 2020 to 2025. The research addressed a critical gap in sustainable finance literature by providing rigorous causal evidence on whether green bonds deliver substantive benefits beyond signaling mechanisms in emerging market contexts.

### 5.1.1. Research Objectives and Scope

The primary objective was to provide a rigorous causal evaluation of how green bond issuance affects corporate performance across both financial and environmental dimensions. The study employed a comprehensive dataset comprising 23,284 firm-year observations from 3,964 ASEAN firms, with 81 treated observations from 20 firms that issued green bonds during the observation window.

The research pursued four specific objectives: (1) systematizing theoretical frameworks linking green finance to corporate performance, (2) empirically measuring causal impacts on financial performance (internal efficiency and market valuation), (3) assessing environmental performance using an authenticity framework to distinguish substantive from symbolic improvements, and (4) evaluating the persistence and statistical stability of these effects through robust econometric methods.

### 5.1.2. Methodological Approach

The study implemented a three-stage identification strategy to address selection bias and confounding. First, Propensity Score Matching (PSM) was employed to construct a comparable control group, given that treated firms were systematically larger, more leveraged, and had higher baseline ESG scores than non-issuers. Second, Difference-in-Differences (DiD) estimation with entity and time fixed effects provided the primary causal identification. Third, System GMM was used as a robustness check to control for dynamic endogeneity and unobservable heterogeneity.

The authenticity framework developed for environmental performance assessment integrated three components: (1) ESG improvement verification (40% weight), (2) third-party certification status (35% weight), and (3) issuer credibility metrics (25% weight). This tiered approach enabled the study to distinguish between procedural compliance and substantive environmental transformation.

### 5.1.3. Research Questions Addressed

The study addressed four critical research questions: (RQ1) whether green bond issuance improves internal profitability (ROA), (RQ2) how market valuation (Tobin's Q) responds to green bond signaling, (RQ3) the extent of environmental improvements and evidence for "material improvement" versus "greenwashing," and (RQ4) the persistence and statistical stability of observed impacts when accounting for dynamic endogeneity.

## 5.2. Key Findings

### 5.2.1. Financial Performance Effects

**Internal Profitability**: The study found no causal evidence that green bond issuance improves accounting-based profitability measured by return on assets. This null result was consistent across all econometric specifications and robust to alternative estimation methods. The finding contradicts expectations that green investments would generate operational cost savings or efficiency gains within the observation window.

**Market Valuation**: Evidence for market premiums was weak and specification-sensitive. While some specifications suggested potential positive market reactions, these effects disappeared when controlling for firm-level characteristics, indicating that positive associations reflect firm selection rather than causal effects of green bond issuance.

**Cost of Debt**: Assessment of whether ASEAN green bond issuers secure preferential financing rates was precluded by severe data limitations in debt cost measures across the sample.

### 5.2.2. Environmental Performance Effects

**ESG Score Improvements**: Treatment effects on environmental, social, and governance scores were positive in direction but statistically insignificant across robust econometric specifications. Apparent positive effects in simpler models were attributable to sector-wide reporting trends rather than issuer-specific improvements following green bond issuance.

**Emissions Intensity**: Analysis of carbon emissions intensity showed directionally consistent evidence of potential environmental benefits, with the most rigorous dynamic panel estimation suggesting marginal significance for emissions reductions. However, the strength of this evidence was limited by statistical significance concerns in alternative specifications.

### 5.2.3. Greenwashing Assessment

The authenticity framework applied to ASEAN green bonds revealed systematic decoupling between certification and impact: nearly all bonds carried third-party certification, while fewer than four percent demonstrated verifiable environmental improvement post-issuance. This pattern indicates widespread greenwashing, where procedural compliance substitutes for substantive environmental transformation.

### 5.2.4. Robustness and Persistence

All econometric estimates passed diagnostic validity tests, confirming the reliability of the identification strategy. Cross-method consistency between different estimation approaches strengthened causal inference, while cohort-specific analysis supported the validity of key identifying assumptions for the majority of testable treatment groups.

## 5.3. Academic Contributions

### 5.3.1. Theoretical Contributions

This study makes three key contributions to sustainable finance theory. First, it provides strong evidence that positive associations between green bond issuance and corporate performance in prior cross-sectional studies reflect **selection effects rather than treatment effects**. The finding that positive coefficients disappear once entity fixed effects control for firm heterogeneity demonstrates the necessity of within-firm panel methods for causal inference in green finance research.

Second, the study challenges the implicit assumption that **certification serves as a credible proxy for environmental impact**. The near-perfect certification rate coexisting with minimal verified improvement demonstrates that use-of-proceeds certification validates process compliance but does not ensure outcome delivery. This finding requires signaling theory to distinguish between credibility signals (certification) and performance signals (verifiable impact).

Third, the null findings for ASEAN contrast with positive effects documented in developed markets, underscoring that **green bond effectiveness is contingent on institutional quality**. The results suggest that enforcement capacity, disclosure mandates, and regulatory stringency moderate green finance impacts, requiring theoretical models to incorporate institutional variables.

### 5.3.2. Methodological Contributions

The study advances econometric methods in sustainable finance research by demonstrating the importance of controlling for selection bias through fixed effects and matching techniques. The three-stage identification strategy (PSM + DiD + System GMM) provides a methodological template for future causal studies in green finance. The authenticity framework offers a practical approach for distinguishing substantive from symbolic environmental improvements in empirical research.

## 5.4. Study Limitations

This study faces several important limitations that constrain interpretation and generalizability. **Treatment sparsity** represents the primary constraint: only 20 of 3,964 firms (0.50%) issued green bonds during the observation window, limiting statistical power and preventing meaningful subgroup analyses. This limitation is inherent to the nascent state of ASEAN green bond markets but necessitates cautious interpretation of null findings.

**Temporal constraints** affect validity in multiple ways. The six-year panel window (2020-2025) may be insufficient to capture long-run effects that materialize over decades. Additionally, the 2020 treatment cohort has zero pre-treatment observations, rendering parallel trends untestable for 25% of treated firms. The short post-treatment windows for recent cohorts (2023-2024) provide minimal time for treatment effects to manifest.

**Data limitations** prevented analysis of key mechanisms. The absence of bond-level yield data precluded formal greenium analysis, while severe sparsity in cost of debt measures (0.7% coverage) prevented testing of the greenium hypothesis. ESG score availability was limited to 17.8% of observations, concentrated among large-cap firms, potentially biasing environmental impact estimates.

**Methodological constraints** include potential violations of identification assumptions. The 2024 cohort showed ESG parallel trends violations, suggesting anticipatory effects or selection dynamics not fully captured by the matching procedure. The authenticity framework, while innovative, relies on existing ESG and certification data that may not capture all dimensions of environmental impact.

**External validity** is limited to the ASEAN context and the 2020-2025 time period. Results may not generalize to developed markets with different institutional frameworks, regulatory environments, or investor sophistication levels. The COVID-19 pandemic's impact on both financial performance and ESG priorities during 2020-2021 represents an additional confounding factor.

## 5.5. Policy and Managerial Implications

### 5.5.1. Regulatory Recommendations for ASEAN

The central policy implication is that **ASEAN green bond standards must transition from process-based to outcome-based certification**. Current frameworks that validate use-of-proceeds without mandating impact verification enable widespread greenwashing. Regulators should implement mandatory post-issuance environmental reporting, performance-based certification renewal, and penalties for non-compliance with environmental targets.

**Enhanced disclosure requirements** should mandate annual emissions reporting, standardized impact metrics, and third-party impact verification. This would enable investors to distinguish between substantive and symbolic environmental commitments, reducing information asymmetry and improving market efficiency.

### 5.5.2. Corporate Strategy Implications

For corporate managers, the findings suggest that **green bonds should be viewed as signaling mechanisms rather than operational transformation catalysts** in the current ASEAN context. Firms seeking reputational benefits and access to ESG-mandated capital pools may benefit from issuance, but should not expect immediate financial performance improvements.

Companies should prioritize **authentic environmental capability building** rather than superficial certification. The Resource-Based View framework suggests that sustainable competitive advantages emerge from rare, inimitable environmental competencies rather than easily replicated certifications.

## 5.6. Future Research Directions

### 5.6.1. Extended Time Horizons

Future research should examine longer-term effects using extended panel data as ASEAN green bond markets mature. Studies with 10-15 year windows could capture delayed environmental and financial benefits that may not manifest within the 6-year observation period.

### 5.6.2. Institutional Moderators

Research should investigate how institutional factors (regulatory stringency, enforcement capacity, investor sophistication) moderate green bond effectiveness. Cross-country comparisons within ASEAN could identify optimal regulatory frameworks for maximizing green bond impact.

### 5.6.3. Firm-Level Heterogeneity

Future studies should explore heterogeneous treatment effects across firm characteristics, sectors, and green project types. Larger sample sizes would enable identification of contexts where green bonds generate positive returns, informing targeted policy interventions.

### 5.6.4. Alternative Identification Strategies

Research using natural experiments, regression discontinuity designs, or randomized controlled trials could strengthen causal identification. Event studies around regulatory changes or certification requirement modifications could provide exogenous variation for impact assessment.

### 5.6.5. Direct Environmental Measurement

Studies incorporating satellite data, facility-level emissions monitoring, or environmental outcome databases could move beyond ESG scores to measure direct environmental impacts. This would provide more precise assessment of whether green bonds achieve their stated environmental objectives.

## 5.7. Conclusion

This study provides the first rigorous causal evidence on green bond impacts in the ASEAN region, contributing to both academic understanding and policy development in sustainable finance. The findings reveal that while ASEAN green bonds serve effective signaling functions, they do not currently generate measurable improvements in financial or environmental performance within observable horizons.

The systematic decoupling between certification (98.5% compliance) and environmental impact (3.9% verified improvement) represents a market failure that requires regulatory intervention. Until certification standards incorporate outcome accountability and performance-based verification, green bond expansion will not reliably translate into environmental benefits.

For ASEAN's sustainable development goals, these findings highlight the urgent need for institutional reform in green finance markets. Transitioning from process-based to outcome-based frameworks, enhancing disclosure requirements, and strengthening enforcement mechanisms are essential for realizing the environmental and economic potential of green bonds in the region.

The study's methodological contributions—demonstrating the importance of controlling for selection effects and developing authenticity frameworks—provide tools for future research in sustainable finance. As green bond markets continue expanding globally, rigorous causal evaluation will be essential for ensuring these instruments deliver their promised environmental and financial benefits.
